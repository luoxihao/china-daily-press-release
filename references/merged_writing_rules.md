# Merged Writing Rules

These rules combine local docx guidance with style tendencies observed from public China Daily articles.

## Core Rule

Write from facts to meaning: first establish what happened, who was involved, where and when it happened, and why it matters; then add background, quotes, cultural explanation, and broader significance.

## Fact And Structure Rules

- Build a fact inventory before drafting.
- Use only confirmed facts in the article body.
- Mark uncertain facts with `[to be confirmed]`, `[source needed]`, or `[official English name to be confirmed]`.
- Put news facts before background explanation.
- Keep paragraphs short, usually one focused idea per paragraph.
- Close information loops: if a program, institution, route, award, or event is mentioned, explain enough for a reader to understand it.
- Use background only when it helps readers understand significance.

## Headline And Lead Rules

- Headlines should be concise and news-like, not slogan-like.
- A headline may use place plus action, event plus outcome, or human-interest feature wording.
- The lead must answer the central news facts.
- Avoid leads that only announce that an event was held.
- Put the strongest verified element near the beginning.

## Quote And Attribution Rules

- Quotes must come from supplied or approved materials.
- Do not invent direct quotes or paraphrased claims.
- Use quotes for experience, expert judgment, human texture, or institutional framing.
- Use paraphrase for routine facts, event details, and long official statements.
- Attribute claims clearly: `according to the organizer`, `the bureau said`, `Zhang said`.

## Data And Date Rules

- Data must have a source or pending-confirmation marker.
- Use `percent`, not `%`.
- Use internationally readable units and conversions when helpful.
- Use simple time expressions when precision is unnecessary, but preserve exact dates for event facts.
- Do not use unsupported superlatives, rankings, economic-impact claims, or future-outcome predictions.

## External Communication Rules

- Explain cultural background to international readers.
- Connect local events to concrete international relevance: participants, exchanges, visitor experience, public fitness, heritage protection, tourism routes, industry cooperation, or city services.
- Use positive but restrained language.
- Avoid Chinese political-slogan translation, tourist-advertising tone, and AI-generated generic wording.
- For sports-tourism stories, combine route, participation, local community, heritage/scenery, and event organization.

## Bilingual Rules

- Chinese and English versions must share the same facts.
- Chinese should be natural news prose, not a literal back-translation.
- English should be rewritten according to English news logic, not Chinese sentence order.
- Keep pending-confirmation markers in both languages.

## Non-Imitation Rule

Use public China Daily articles only as a style research base. Never copy full text, distinctive phrasing, or structure from a specific article. The final draft must be newly written from user-provided facts.
