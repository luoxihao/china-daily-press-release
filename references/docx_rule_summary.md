# Docx Rule Summary

Source: `新闻采写docx.docx`

## Mandatory Rules

- Build the fact list and angle before writing.
- Use verified names, titles, organizations, dates, locations, and data.
- Use direct quotes only from supplied or approved source material.
- Mark uncertain translations, official English names, titles, and data as pending confirmation.
- Keep English paragraphs short and functional.
- Remove unsupported praise, slogan language, first-person promotion, and vague future promises.
- Explain Chinese cultural, historical, administrative, and event context for international readers.

## Recommended Rules

- Choose the strongest news point for the lead rather than the activity name.
- Use foreign experts, foreign participants, and specialist voices when they clarify international relevance.
- Place key facts before background and significance.
- Use data to support meaning, but explain the data in reader-friendly terms.
- Use a restrained, objective-positive tone.
- For bilingual drafts, preserve the same fact base while allowing natural language differences.

## Forbidden Rules

- Do not fabricate quotes, people, affiliations, organizers, titles, numbers, schedules, awards, or outcomes.
- Do not use "Mr" for routine English news attribution.
- Do not write `%`; use `percent`.
- Do not use local Chinese shorthand without explanation.
- Do not leave an introduced fact unexplained.
- Do not write a lead that only says an activity was held unless that is the central news.
- Do not copy source release headers, logos, or irrelevant promotional sections into the article.

## Terminology Rules

- Use `United States` at first mention, not `US` only.
- Use hectares for land area when writing for international readers.
- Convert yuan to US dollars on first mention if money is editorially relevant.
- Use lowercase for generic administrative terms: `city`, `district`, `county`, `province`.
- Preserve proper names such as `Binhai New Area`.
- Avoid ambiguous abbreviations such as `NPC`.

## Headline Rules

- Prefer concise, news-like headlines.
- Highlight action, outcome, place, or verified significance.
- Avoid empty slogans, subjective praise, or tourism-advertising claims.
- Do not overpack full official event names into the headline.

## Lead Rules

- Use one or two sentences.
- Answer the central news question: who did what, where, when, why it matters, and who is affected.
- Put the strongest verified element first.
- Avoid "X event was held in Y" when a more meaningful angle exists.

## Translation Rules

- Rewrite Chinese materials into English news logic; do not translate sentence by sentence.
- Use official English names where available; otherwise mark pending confirmation.
- Keep Chinese annotations for uncertain names or titles during editing.
- Avoid Chinese political slogan translation and officialese.

## Fact-Checking Rules

- Data, definitions, specialized claims, and historical background require sources.
- Direct quotes need speaker identity, title, and source status.
- Conflicting source facts must be listed instead of resolved by guessing.
- Claims about future impact must be supported by concrete plans or attributed statements.

## External Communication Rules

- Connect local events to international readers through people, participation, heritage, trade, tourism, sports, culture, or shared experience.
- Explain context without overloading the lead.
- Use positive but restrained language.
- Use photo captions and credits carefully when media assets are included.

## Chinese-English Interference Rules

- Replace long official event names with a short first reference plus explanation.
- Avoid "grandly held", "successfully held", "vigorously promote", "inject vitality", and similar literal promotional translations.
- Prefer active, concrete verbs and source-backed nouns.
- Delete repeated value judgments unless they come from a verified quote.
